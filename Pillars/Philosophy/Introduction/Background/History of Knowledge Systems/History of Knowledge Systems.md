# History of Knowledge Systems

## Overview

The impulse to preserve knowledge - to carry it beyond the confines of a single mind and the limitations of a single life - is not uniquely
human. It is older than language, older than civilisation, older than consciousness itself. What humans have done is accelerate and
elaborate on a project that life began long before us: how do we make what is learned now available later, to those who were not there when
it was learned?

---

## Nature's Answer

Before language, before writing, before memory, life had already solved a version of this problem. DNA is the oldest knowledge system on
earth - three and a half billion years of accumulated learning about how to survive, encoded in a molecule and copied faithfully forward
with every generation. Every successful organism is a record of what worked. Every strand of DNA is a compressed archive of ancestral
experience, refined by an unimaginably long process of trial, failure, and survival.

But genetic knowledge is slow. It can only encode what is heritable, and it can only update on evolutionary timescales. It cannot transmit
within a lifetime the discovery that a particular berry is poisonous, that the river floods in autumn, or that the neighbouring tribe can be
trusted. For that, a faster medium was needed.

---

## The Spoken Word

The human breakthrough was symbolic knowledge: the ability to encode experience in language and transmit it within a lifetime, across a
community, and down the generations. Before writing, this was the only mechanism available. Oral tradition - story, song, fable, saga,
myth - carried everything a community needed to survive and flourish.

The forms were not arbitrary. Fables and parables compress moral lessons into memorable narrative because narrative survives memory where
raw instruction does not. The Norse sagas preserved genealogy, geography, and law across centuries without a written word. The griot
traditions of West Africa carried history, social structure, and collective identity in living voice. The Iliad encoded military tactics and
aristocratic values in metre because rhythm is easier to remember than prose.

The strength of oral tradition is that it is alive - adaptive, community-owned, responsive to context. Its fragility is the same: it dies
with its carriers. When a community is destroyed, scattered, or simply stops telling its stories, the knowledge ends with it.

---

## The First Externalisation

Writing changed the relationship between knowledge and mortality. When knowledge could be inscribed on clay, papyrus, or stone, it no longer
depended on human memory or community continuity. For the first time, knowledge could genuinely outlive its knower.

It is worth noting that writing begins not with philosophy but with grain records. The earliest Sumerian tablets are administrative -
inventories, accounts, allocations. Knowledge systems start as practical tools. The preservation of wisdom is built on the infrastructure of
the ledger.

---

## The Libraries

Once knowledge could be written, it could be collected. The great libraries - Alexandria, the House of Wisdom in Baghdad, Ashurbanipal's
library at Nineveh - represent the first systematic attempt to gather everything known. The ambition was total: all knowledge, under one
roof, available to those who sought it.

The catastrophe was equally total. Everything in one place can be lost in one fire. Alexandria burned - partially, repeatedly, across
centuries - and with it went works no one has read since. The single point of failure is as old as the library itself.

---

## Survival Through Distribution

The response to catastrophe was distribution. Medieval monasteries functioned as knowledge nodes: scriptoriums copying manuscripts, each
house a partial redundant record of the corpus. The Islamic Golden Age served as a transfer network - preserving and translating Greek texts
that would otherwise have been lost to Europe, returning them enriched with Arabic mathematics, astronomy, and medicine.

The printing press changed the scale entirely. When Gutenberg made reproduction cheap, centralised destruction ceased to be fatal. Too many
copies existed to destroy. Knowledge escaped the hands of the few. The new problem was not scarcity but abundance: too much to absorb,
curation becoming the new constraint.

---

## Organisation and Retrieval

The 19th and early 20th centuries grappled with the flood. Dewey's decimal system, the card catalogue, the index - all attempts to impose
retrievable structure on an expanding corpus. Paul Otlet, working in Brussels in the 1930s, built the Mundaneum: a global knowledge network
from index cards, anticipating the web by sixty years.

Niklas Luhmann's Zettelkasten articulated a different insight: the connections between ideas matter as much as the ideas themselves. His
card index - tens of thousands of notes in intricate cross-reference - produced a body of work no single mind could have held. The network
was the knowledge.

---

## The Digital Turn

The web distributed knowledge at a scale the printing press could not have imagined. Search made the corpus navigable. Wikipedia
demonstrated that collective open authorship could produce something both vast and reasonably reliable. And then large language models
arrived - systems that do not merely store knowledge but traverse and synthesise across it, generating answers to questions no human has
explicitly written down.

This introduces a new fragility. When generation is cheap, provenance becomes the scarcity. When synthesis is instant, the canonical
record - the authoritative, trusted version - becomes harder to maintain and more important to have. We have more knowledge available than
at any point in history, and less certainty about what to trust.

The question each era has faced remains: how do we make what is known now available later, to those who were not there when it was learned?
The answers have grown more sophisticated. The problem has not gone away.
