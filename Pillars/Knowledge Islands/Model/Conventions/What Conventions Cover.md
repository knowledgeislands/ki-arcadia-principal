# What Conventions Cover

## Overview

Conventions are what make an island coherent over time. Without them, notes accumulate inconsistently, knowledge becomes hard to find, and
the island loses the property that makes it useful - that any agent, human or artificial, can navigate it reliably and contribute to it
without ambiguity.

Three areas of convention govern how the island operates. Together they answer three questions about any piece of knowledge: what does it
look like, where does it live, and where does it belong? Any note, agent, or tool interacting with the island should conform to all three.

---

## Notes

Notes defines the format and type system for island content - frontmatter properties, tags, note types, and the structural conventions that
make notes machine-readable and human-navigable. It is the primary reference for anyone authoring or reviewing island content, and the first
stop for understanding what a well-formed note looks like.

## Residency

Residency defines what it means for knowledge to belong on an island - the criteria that distinguish notes that should be written and
maintained here from content that belongs elsewhere. It prevents the island from accumulating material that dilutes its signal or duplicates
what is better held in external systems.

## Structure

Structure defines the library layout - the four top-level folders, their boundaries, the routing rules for new content, and the conventions
for index notes and folder naming. It is the reference for anyone navigating, extending, or restructuring the island.
